Answer the following Canadian regulatory compliance question clearly and practically: $ARGUMENTS

## Your Task

Provide a clear, well-structured answer that helps a business/operations team member understand what the rules are and what they need to do. You are not providing legal advice — you are explaining what the law says and how it applies in practical terms.

## Answer Format

### Direct Answer
Start with a clear, one-to-three sentence answer to the question. Don't bury the bottom line.

### Explanation
Explain the relevant rules in plain language. Cite the specific legislation and section where applicable (e.g., "Under the Ontario ESA, s.57..."). Cover:
- What the rule is
- Who it applies to
- What action it requires (or prohibits)
- Any important exceptions or conditions

### Jurisdiction Notes
If the answer differs by province or between federal and provincial law, clearly explain the differences. Default coverage: Federal, Ontario, BC, and Alberta.

### Practical Guidance
What should the person actually do based on this information? Give specific, actionable steps.

### Caveats
- Note any areas of legal uncertainty or where the answer depends on facts not provided
- Remind the user that this is general information, not legal advice, and recommend consulting a lawyer for high-stakes decisions

### Useful Resources
Link to the relevant regulator or government resource where the user can find more information:
- Canada Labour Code, ESA Ontario, etc.: canlii.org (free Canadian legal database)
- PIPEDA / OPC: priv.gc.ca
- FINTRAC: fintrac-canafe.gc.ca
- Ontario Ministry of Labour: ontario.ca/labour
- WorkSafeBC: worksafebc.com
- Alberta OHS: alberta.ca/ohs
- CCOHS: ccohs.ca

---

Calibrate your answer to the complexity of the question. Simple factual questions (e.g., "What is the minimum wage in BC?") get a concise answer. Complex questions (e.g., "Can I terminate an employee who is on medical leave?") get a thorough analysis with all relevant nuances.

Always be honest about uncertainty — Canadian employment and privacy law contains genuinely unsettled areas, and it is better to flag this than to overstate confidence.
