# Canadian Workplace Health & Safety Compliance

## Governing Legislation by Jurisdiction

| Jurisdiction | Legislation | Regulator |
|-------------|------------|---------|
| Federal | Canada Occupational Health and Safety Regulations (under Part II of the Canada Labour Code) | Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC) / Labour Program |
| Ontario | Occupational Health and Safety Act (OHSA) | Ontario Ministry of Labour, Immigration, Training and Skills Development |
| BC | Workers Compensation Act + OHS Regulation (WorkSafeBC) | WorkSafeBC |
| Alberta | Occupational Health and Safety Act (AB OHS Act) + OHS Code | OHS—Alberta (Employment Standards and Workplace Safety) |

## Core Employer Obligations (All Jurisdictions)

Every Canadian jurisdiction requires employers to:
1. Take every reasonable precaution to protect workers from workplace hazards.
2. Establish and enforce safety policies and procedures.
3. Provide information, instruction, supervision, and training necessary to protect worker safety.
4. Maintain equipment, materials, and premises in safe condition.
5. Investigate workplace incidents, injuries, and near-misses.
6. Report serious incidents to the relevant authority.
7. Maintain a joint health and safety committee (JHSC) or health and safety representative, depending on workplace size.

## Joint Health and Safety Committees (JHSC) / Representatives

| Jurisdiction | JHSC Required | H&S Rep Required |
|-------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Federal | 20+ employees at workplace | <20 employees |
| Ontario | 20+ employees at workplace, or designated industries | <20 employees |
| BC | 20+ employees at workplace | <20 employees |
| Alberta | 20+ employees at workplace | <20 employees |

**JHSC composition:** Co-chaired by management and worker representatives; at least half of members must be workers not exercising managerial functions.
**Meeting frequency:** At minimum quarterly (monthly for higher-risk workplaces in some jurisdictions).
**Certified members:** Ontario and BC require at least one management and one worker member to hold joint certification.

## WHMIS 2015 (Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System)

WHMIS 2015 is aligned with the **Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals (GHS)** and is implemented through:
- **Federal:** Hazardous Products Act (HPA) and Hazardous Products Regulations (HPR) — govern suppliers
- **Provincial OH&S legislation** — govern employers in the workplace

### Hazard Classes (WHMIS 2015)

**Physical Hazards:**
Flammable gases, aerosols, oxidizing gases, gases under pressure, flammable liquids, flammable solids, self-reactive substances, pyrophoric liquids/solids, self-heating substances, substances that emit flammable gas on contact with water, oxidizing liquids/solids, organic peroxides, corrosive to metals, combustible dusts, simple asphyxiants.

**Health Hazards:**
Acute toxicity, skin corrosion/irritation, serious eye damage/irritation, respiratory or skin sensitization, germ cell mutagenicity, carcinogenicity, reproductive toxicity, specific target organ toxicity (single exposure), specific target organ toxicity (repeated exposure), aspiration hazard.

**Environmental Hazards:**
Aquatic toxicity (chronic/acute) — note: not yet required on workplace labels but may be on SDS.

### Labels

**Supplier labels** must include:
- Product identifier
- Supplier name and address
- Hazard pictogram(s)
- Signal word ("Danger" or "Warning")
- Hazard statement(s)
- Precautionary statement(s)
- Supplemental label information as required

**Workplace labels** (when product decanted into another container) must include:
- Product identifier
- Safe handling information
- Reference to the SDS

### Safety Data Sheets (SDS)

SDS must follow the 16-section GHS format:

1. Identification
2. Hazard identification
3. Composition / information on ingredients
4. First-aid measures
5. Fire-fighting measures
6. Accidental release measures
7. Handling and storage
8. Exposure controls / personal protection
9. Physical and chemical properties
10. Stability and reactivity
11. Toxicological information
12. Ecological information
13. Disposal considerations
14. Transport information
15. Regulatory information
16. Other information (including SDS preparation/revision date)

**Employer obligations:** Obtain an SDS for every controlled product; make SDS readily accessible to workers at all times during a shift; update SDS when new hazard information is received.

## Workers' Three Fundamental Rights

All Canadian jurisdictions recognize three fundamental worker rights:

1. **Right to Know** — Workers have the right to know about hazards in the workplace (WHMIS, SDS, training).
2. **Right to Participate** — Workers have the right to participate in identifying and resolving safety concerns (JHSC, health and safety representatives).
3. **Right to Refuse Unsafe Work** — Workers have the right to refuse work they have reasonable cause to believe is dangerous to themselves or others.

### Refusal to Work Process (Ontario OHSA)
1. Worker reports concern to supervisor.
2. Supervisor investigates in the presence of the worker and a JHSC member.
3. If dispute persists, Ministry of Labour inspector is called.
4. Inspector investigates and issues a decision.
5. Worker may not be penalized for exercising the right of refusal in good faith.

## Workplace Incident Reporting Requirements

### Critical Injuries / Fatalities (report immediately)
All jurisdictions require **immediate notification** (telephone or in person) to the regulator when:
- A worker is killed
- A worker sustains a critical injury (defined as: fracture of skull/spine/pelvis/arm/leg; amputation of an arm/leg/hand/foot; loss of sight; severe burns; unconsciousness)
- There is a structural failure or explosion

**Do not disturb the scene** (except to assist injured workers or prevent further injury) until authorized by an inspector.

### Written Reports
- Ontario OHSA: written report within 48 hours for critical injury/fatality; Form 7 to WSIB within 3 days of employer learning of injury requiring healthcare beyond first aid
- BC WorkSafeBC: serious injury/incident investigation report within 48 hours; investigation report within 30 days
- Alberta: immediate notification for serious incidents; investigation report within prescribed time

### Near-Miss Reporting
All jurisdictions encourage near-miss reporting (incidents with potential for injury but where no injury occurred). Near-miss investigation is a leading indicator of safety performance. Establish a no-blame reporting culture.

## Due Diligence Defense

An employer facing a regulatory charge may avoid liability by demonstrating **due diligence** — that the employer took every reasonable precaution in the circumstances to prevent the incident. Evidence supporting due diligence includes:
- Written safety policies and procedures
- Regular workplace inspections
- Worker training records
- Maintenance and equipment inspection logs
- JHSC meeting minutes and action items
- Documented investigation of prior incidents and corrective actions
- Supervisor accountability mechanisms
