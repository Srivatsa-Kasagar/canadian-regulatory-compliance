# Canadian Corporate Governance

## Governing Legislation

| Jurisdiction | Legislation | Regulator |
|-------------|------------|---------|
| Federal | Canada Business Corporations Act (CBCA) | Corporations Canada (Innovation, Science and Economic Development Canada) |
| Ontario | Business Corporations Act (OBCA) | Ontario Business Registry |
| British Columbia | Business Corporations Act (BCBCA) | BC Registry Services |
| Alberta | Business Corporations Act (ABCA) | Alberta Corporate Registry |

**Which law applies?** A corporation is governed by the jurisdiction in which it is incorporated — not where it operates. A federally incorporated company follows the CBCA regardless of which province it operates in. Choose federal incorporation if operating in multiple provinces; provincial incorporation if primarily operating in one province.

## Director Duties

Directors of Canadian corporations owe two core duties:

### 1. Fiduciary Duty (Duty of Loyalty)
- Act honestly and in good faith **with a view to the best interests of the corporation**
- Avoid conflicts of interest — disclose any material interest in a contract or transaction to the board
- Do not use corporate information or opportunities for personal gain
- Applies to all directors; cannot be contracted out of

**Conflict of interest procedure (CBCA s.120):**
1. Director discloses the material interest at the next board meeting (or in writing)
2. Interested director must **not vote** on the resolution
3. Disclosure and abstention are recorded in minutes

### 2. Duty of Care (Duty of Diligence)
- Exercise the care, diligence, and skill that a **reasonably prudent person** would exercise in comparable circumstances
- Be informed: attend meetings, review financial statements, ask questions
- The standard is objective — what a reasonably prudent person would do, not what the director actually did

**Business Judgment Rule:** Courts will defer to a director's decision if the director: (a) acted on an informed basis, (b) in good faith, (c) in the best interests of the corporation. This protects well-intentioned decisions from hindsight scrutiny.

### Director Liability Exposure
Directors can be personally liable for:
- **Unpaid wages and vacation pay** (CBCA s.119 / provincial ESA equivalents) — up to 6 months wages
- **Unremitted source deductions** (CPP, EI, income tax) — the Crown has priority
- **HST/GST unpaid by the corporation** — CRA can pursue directors if corporation fails to remit
- **Environmental cleanup orders** (in some circumstances)
- **Securities law violations** (misrepresentation in disclosure documents)

**Due diligence defence:** A director is not liable for unpaid wages/taxes if they exercised reasonable care to prevent the failure — document active oversight of financial obligations.

## Corporate Records — Minute Book Requirements

Every Canadian corporation must maintain a **minute book** (paper or electronic) at its registered office or designated records office. Required records:

| Record | CBCA Requirement |
|--------|-----------------|
| Articles of Incorporation | Permanent |
| By-laws (current and historical) | Permanent |
| Register of Directors (current + historical) | Permanent |
| Register of Officers | Permanent |
| Register of Shareholders | Current + 6 years after transfer |
| Register of Securities | Permanent |
| Unanimous Shareholder Agreements | Current version |
| Minutes of shareholder meetings | Permanent |
| Minutes of board meetings | Permanent |
| Minutes of committee meetings | Permanent |
| Financial statements (last 6 years) | 6 years |
| Proxies and voting records | Until next annual meeting |

**Beneficial Ownership Register (new CBCA requirement — in force 2023):**
Corporations must maintain a register of **individuals with significant control (ISC)** — those who directly or indirectly own or control 25%+ of voting shares or 25%+ of the fair market value of all shares. See Beneficial Ownership section below.

## Beneficial Ownership Register (ISC Register)

### Who Must Comply
All CBCA corporations (private and public, except reporting issuers and certain listed entities) must maintain and file an ISC register.

### Definition: Individual with Significant Control
An individual who:
- Directly or indirectly owns or controls **25% or more of voting shares**
- Directly or indirectly owns or controls shares representing **25% or more of the FMV of all outstanding shares**
- Has direct or indirect influence resulting in **de facto control** over the corporation

### Information Required in the Register
- Full legal name
- Date of birth
- Latest known address
- Jurisdiction of residence for tax purposes
- Date on which they became or ceased to be an ISC
- Description of how they qualify as an ISC (ownership, control, or influence)

### Filing Obligations (CBCA Amendment 2023)
- Corporations must **file their ISC register** with Corporations Canada annually (within 15 days of the annual meeting or June 15, whichever comes first for non-annual updates)
- Corporations must update the register within **15 days** of becoming aware of a change
- **Penalties** for non-compliance: up to $200,000 for the corporation; directors and officers liable up to $200,000 or 6 months imprisonment

## Annual Filings

### Federal (CBCA) — Annual Return
- **File with:** Corporations Canada
- **Deadline:** Within **60 days** of the anniversary of the corporation's incorporation date
- **Contents:** Confirmation/update of registered office address, directors, and officers
- **Fee:** $12–$40 online (varies by type)
- **Penalty for late filing:** The corporation can be dissolved (struck off) for failure to file

### Ontario (OBCA) — Annual Return
- **File with:** Ontario Business Registry
- **Deadline:** 6 months after fiscal year-end
- **Extra:** Ontario corporations must also file notice of change for director/officer changes within 15 days

### BC (BCBCA) — Annual Report
- **File with:** BC Registry Services
- **Deadline:** Within 2 months of the corporation's **recognition date anniversary** (i.e., anniversary of incorporation)

### Alberta (ABCA) — Annual Return
- **File with:** Alberta Corporate Registry
- **Deadline:** Within 3 months of fiscal year-end
- **Extra:** Must maintain current registered address and agent

## Shareholder Rights

### Information Rights
- Shareholders may inspect corporate records (register of shareholders, minutes of shareholder meetings, financial statements) on request
- Annual financial statements must be provided to shareholders before or at the annual meeting

### Voting Rights
- Ordinary resolutions: majority of votes cast (>50%)
- Special resolutions: two-thirds of votes cast (required for: articles amendment, amalgamation, continuance, sale of substantially all assets, dissolution)
- Class votes: required for amendments affecting a specific class of shares

### Dissent Rights (CBCA s.190)
Shareholders may dissent from certain fundamental changes (amalgamation, articles amendment affecting their class, continuance to another jurisdiction, sale of substantially all assets) and demand the corporation pay fair value for their shares.

### Oppression Remedy (CBCA s.241)
Any complainant (shareholder, creditor, director, officer) can apply to court if the corporation's conduct is:
- **Oppressive** — unfairly disregards the complainant's interests
- **Unfairly prejudicial** — treats the complainant in a manner that is unjust
- **Unfairly disregards** their interests

The court can make any order it sees fit: winding up, share purchase, injunction, or damages. The oppression remedy is the most powerful tool for minority shareholders.

### Derivative Actions (CBCA s.239)
Shareholders or directors may apply to court to bring an action on behalf of the corporation (e.g., to sue a director for breach of fiduciary duty) when management refuses to act. Court must be satisfied the action is in the best interests of the corporation.

## Audit Committee Requirements

**Publicly listed companies** must have an audit committee under National Instrument 52-110:
- Minimum 3 members; all must be **independent**
- Majority must be **financially literate**
- Mandate: oversee external audit, review financial statements, review internal controls

**Private CBCA corporations:** No mandatory audit committee, but governance best practices recommend one for corporations with significant revenues or external debt.
