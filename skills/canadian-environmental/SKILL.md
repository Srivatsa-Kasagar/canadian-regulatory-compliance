# Canadian Environmental Compliance

## Key Legislation

| Legislation | Jurisdiction | Scope |
|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Canadian Environmental Protection Act (CEPA 1999) | Federal | Toxic substances, pollution prevention, environmental emergencies |
| Impact Assessment Act (IAA, 2019) | Federal | Federal environmental assessment of designated projects |
| Greenhouse Gas Pollution Pricing Act (GGPPA) | Federal | Carbon pricing backstop |
| Transportation of Dangerous Goods Act (TDG) | Federal | Classification, handling, and transport of dangerous goods |
| Fisheries Act | Federal | Protection of fish and fish habitat; prohibition on harmful deposits |

| BC Environmental Management Act (EMA) | BC | Provincial discharge permits, contaminated sites, waste |
| Alberta Environmental Protection and Enhancement Act (EPEA) | Alberta | Provincial approvals, waste, contaminated sites |

## Carbon Pricing in Canada

Canada operates a two-track carbon pricing system. Provinces/territories can adopt their own system — if it meets federal benchmarks, the federal backstop does not apply.

### Federal Backstop: Two Components

**1. Fuel Charge (consumer-facing)**
- Applies to fossil fuels (gasoline, diesel, natural gas, propane, etc.)
- Paid by fuel producers/distributors; cost passed to consumers
- Rate for 2024: $80/tonne CO₂e → increases to $170/tonne by 2030
- Applies in provinces without an equivalent system: currently Ontario, Manitoba, New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, PEI, Newfoundland & Labrador, Saskatchewan, and territories
- Most revenue returned to households via the Canada Carbon Rebate

**2. Output-Based Pricing System (OBPS)**
- Applies to **large industrial facilities** emitting 50,000+ tonnes CO₂e/year (or 10,000+ tonnes for those that opt in)
- Sets an output-based standard (performance benchmark per unit of output)
- Facilities emitting above the standard pay a charge on excess emissions; facilities below earn credits they can sell or bank
- Compliance units: surplus credits, eligible offset credits, or payment to the OBPS fund
- Facilities must register, report emissions, and submit compliance units annually

### Provincial Carbon Pricing Systems (Meeting Federal Benchmark)

**British Columbia — BC Carbon Tax**
- Canada's oldest carbon tax (since 2008)
- Rate mirrors the federal trajectory: $80/tonne in 2024, rising to $170/tonne by 2030
- Applies to combustion of fossil fuels; revenue recycled via tax reductions and credits
- BC's industrial facilities covered by the BC OBPS (Reporting Regulation)

**Alberta — TIER (Technology Innovation and Emissions Reduction)**
- Applies to facilities emitting 100,000+ tonnes CO₂e/year (opt-in at 10,000+ tonnes)
- Performance-standard model: facilities exceeding their benchmark pay into the TIER fund or purchase offset credits
- Current benchmark: 90% of sector average (improves annually)
- TIER fund used for clean technology projects

**Quebec — Cap-and-Trade (Western Climate Initiative)**
- Linked with California's cap-and-trade system
- Applies to industrial emitters, electricity importers, fuel distributors
- Companies must surrender allowances equal to their emissions

**Other provinces:** Ontario, Manitoba use the federal OBPS for large industry and federal fuel charge.

## Environmental Assessment

### Federal — Impact Assessment Act (IAA, 2019)
The IAA replaced CEAA 2012. Federal EA is required for **designated projects** listed in the Physical Activities Regulations.

**Designated projects include (selected):**
- Mines producing 5,000+ tonnes/day
- Oil sands projects 10,000+ m³/day
- Pipelines 40+ km in length
- Nuclear facilities
- Offshore oil/gas projects
- Certain hydroelectric facilities, bridges, international power lines

**IA process:**
1. Proponent submits Initial Project Description → Impact Assessment Agency of Canada (IAAC) screens for designation
2. Planning phase: IAAC identifies issues, indigenous consultation begins
3. Impact Assessment phase: proponent submits Impact Statement; public and expert review
4. Decision phase: Minister (or Governor in Council for major projects) makes final decision
5. Post-decision: conditions and follow-up monitoring

### Provincial Environmental Assessment Triggers

| Province | Legislation | Key Triggers |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Ontario | Environmental Assessment Act | Designated undertakings (transit, waste, hydro projects); Class EAs for routine projects |
| BC | Environmental Assessment Act | Projects in prescribed categories by size (mines, energy, water, waste, industrial) |
| Alberta | EPEA + Responsible Energy Development Act | Energy projects via AER; non-energy projects via AEP |

**Coordination:** Federal and provincial EAs may be conducted jointly to avoid duplication.

## Hazardous Waste and Dangerous Goods

### Transportation of Dangerous Goods (TDG)
The federal TDG Act regulates the transport of dangerous goods by road, rail, air, and water.

**Key obligations:**
- **Classification:** Classify materials using the TDG classification system (9 classes including explosives, flammable liquids, toxic substances, corrosives, etc.)
- **Shipping documents:** Provide accurate shipping documents identifying the dangerous goods
- **Labels and placards:** Apply required labels to containers and placards to vehicles
- **Emergency Response Assistance Plans (ERAPs):** Required for certain high-risk substances
- **Training:** Anyone who handles, offers for transport, or transports dangerous goods must be trained and certified
- **Emergency reporting:** Report accidents/incidents involving dangerous goods to CANUTEC (1-888-CANUTEC)

### Provincial Hazardous Waste (Manifesting)

| Province | System | Key Requirement |
|---------|--------|----------------|
| Ontario | Electronic Hazardous Waste Manifest (EHW) | Generators, carriers, and receivers must use the provincial manifest system |
| BC | BC Hazardous Waste Regulation | Licensed carriers; manifest system; storage limits and facility approvals |
| Alberta | Hazardous Waste Regulation (EPEA) | Generator registration; manifest for transport; licensed disposal facilities only |

**Cradle-to-grave responsibility:** Generators remain responsible for hazardous waste until it is properly disposed of. Improper disposal can result in significant civil and regulatory liability.

## Spill Reporting Requirements

### Federal (Fisheries Act, s.38 / CEPA)
- **Immediate reporting** to Environment and Climate Change Canada if a spill of a polluting substance reaches or may reach a waterway
- Report to Transport Canada for TDG incidents involving dangerous goods

### Ontario (OEPA, s.15)
- **Immediate reporting** to the Spills Action Centre (1-800-268-6060) for any spill that causes or is likely to cause an adverse effect
- Owner/operator must take immediate clean-up action
- Written report required within prescribed timeframe for significant spills

### BC (EMA)
- Immediate reporting to the BC Ministry of Environment Emergency line
- Environmental emergency: 1-800-663-3456

### Alberta (EPEA)
- Immediate reporting to the Alberta Environment and Protected Areas Emergency Line (1-800-222-6514)

## CEPA Toxic Substances

CEPA Schedule 1 lists substances deemed toxic. If your business manufactures, uses, imports, or releases Schedule 1 substances:
- Check if a **Pollution Prevention (P2) Plan** is required
- Check for **Environmental Performance Agreements** or industry-specific codes of practice
- Report under the **National Pollutant Release Inventory (NPRI)** if your facility meets reporting thresholds for listed substances
